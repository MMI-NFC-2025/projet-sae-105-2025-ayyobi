Discoveries and achievements

Computing the return of Halley’s Comet (1759)

Nicole-Reine Lepaute worked with astronomers Clairaut and Lalande to compute the precise date of Halley’s Comet return.

She spent months doing manual calculations, correcting errors and refining trajectories. Thanks to their work, the comet was observed almost exactly on the predicted date—an outstanding scientific feat for the time.

Writing the Treatise on Clockmaking (1755–1757)

She collaborated with her husband Jean-André Lepaute, the King’s clockmaker, on a treatise about time measurement and astronomical clocks.

Her mathematical knowledge helped improve the precision of pendulum clocks used for celestial observations, at a time when timekeeping was central to scientific progress.

Computing astronomical ephemerides (1760–1774)

Nicole-Reine Lepaute contributed to the production of official astronomical tables. Ephemerides provide the daily positions of planets and stars—useful for navigators, astronomers and engineers.

She devoted many years to meticulous work, often behind the scenes. This mechanism of erasure is precisely what the Matilda Effect describes.

Studies of eclipses and celestial phenomena

She revised predictions for solar and lunar eclipses, improving calculation methods. Her work shows scientific rigor and mastery of celestial mechanics.

“She calculated the stars, but History forgot her name.”

— Closing line (Matilda mockup)

Collaboration with Jérôme Lalande

Throughout her career, Nicole-Reine collaborated with Jérôme Lalande, one of France’s greatest astronomers. She assisted in writing his works and in research on planetary orbits.

Her role—often discreet—was essential to the success of these projects, and this site aims to make it visible.

ABOUT

My name is Nargis Ayyobi, a student in MMi in Montbéliard.

I chose Nicole-Reine Lepaute, a pioneer of astronomy, as a symbol of perseverance and knowledge.
